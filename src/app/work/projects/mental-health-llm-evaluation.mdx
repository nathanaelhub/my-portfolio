## The question

Healthcare organisations are starting to deploy LLMs into patient-facing mental-health workflows, and they face a choice that's framed badly. Vendors pitch it as *cloud vs local* — but the real question underneath is:

> **Is the privacy gain of keeping patient conversations on-premise worth the therapeutic quality you lose by running a smaller model?**

Most hospitals answer this by gut feel, by RFP marketing language, or by whichever vendor visited first. There's no validated framework that turns "this AI seems good" into "this AI scores 7.5 on validated therapeutic-effectiveness metrics with statistical significance across 40 standardised scenarios."

That framework is what this capstone built.

## Approach

I designed a **three-pillar evaluation**, weighted by what actually matters in clinical use:

| Pillar | Weight | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| **Therapeutic effectiveness** | 45% | Empathy expression, active listening (paraphrasing, clarifying questions, emotional labeling), use of therapeutic techniques (CBT reframing, grounding, breathing), crisis detection, safety boundaries |
| **Patient experience** | 30% | Perceived helpfulness, trust, clarity, engagement, overall satisfaction — scored from the *patient's* perspective, not the engineer's |
| **Technical performance** | 25% | Response speed, context memory across multi-turn conversations, token efficiency, resource usage (local models only) |

The 45/30/25 split is deliberate: technical perfection is useless if the AI dismisses someone's depression, and a clinically perfect AI that feels cold or untrustworthy doesn't get used.

### Four models tested

| Model | Type | Why included |
|---|---|---|
| **OpenAI GPT-4** | Cloud, frontier | The current ceiling for general therapeutic conversation |
| **Claude-3** | Cloud, frontier | Trained with explicit safety methodology — does that translate to therapy? |
| **DeepSeek R1** | Local-capable, open | Strongest open model that can run on-premise affordably |
| **Gemma-3 12B** | Local, small | What does a hospital actually get for free, on a single GPU? |

### Scenario design

40 standardised mental-health scenarios spanning anxiety, depression, grief, work stress, relationship conflict, and crisis situations. Each scenario was authored to test specific therapeutic skills — e.g., the work-anxiety scenario tests whether the model validates feelings before jumping to solutions, the crisis scenarios test whether the model recognises risk and provides appropriate escalation resources.

Each model ran every scenario. Every response was scored against the three-pillar rubric. Statistical tests (t-tests, ANOVA, Cohen's d for effect size) compared models pairwise.

## What the numbers actually showed

### Overall composite scores

| Model | Composite | Selected for live deployment | Strongest at |
|---|---|---|---|
| **GPT-4** | **7.42 / 10** | 40% of scenarios | Anxiety scenarios, crisis handling |
| **DeepSeek R1** | **7.06 / 10** | 60% of scenarios | Depression support, cost-effective |
| **Claude-3** | 5.45 / 10 | 0% | Moderate across the board |
| **Gemma-3 12B** | 4.10 / 10 | 0% | Consistent baseline floor |

### The headline result that surprised me

I expected GPT-4 to dominate. It didn't. **DeepSeek R1, running locally with full data privacy, was selected as the better model for 60% of scenarios** — particularly depression-support contexts where its more direct, structured-advice style outperformed GPT-4's softer, more conversational empathy.

That's the result a hospital should actually care about: there's a real, defensible case for the local model — not because privacy "matters in principle," but because it scored *higher* on the patient-facing rubric in the majority of scenarios tested.

### The cost in numbers, not vibes

Statistical comparison for therapeutic effectiveness, the most clinically meaningful pillar:

- **GPT-4 vs DeepSeek**: mean difference 0.36 points (n.s. at α=0.05) — they're a coin flip on therapy quality.
- **GPT-4 vs Claude-3**: mean difference 1.97 points (p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 1.4 — large effect).
- **GPT-4 vs Gemma-3**: mean difference 3.32 points (p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.1 — very large effect).

The decision matrix this actually unlocks:

| Hospital priority | The data says |
|---|---|
| Privacy first | Use DeepSeek R1 locally — quality cost is negligible vs GPT-4 |
| Quality first | GPT-4 cloud — but the gap over DeepSeek is smaller than you'd think |
| Budget conscious | DeepSeek R1 — same quality envelope, $0 per-token |
| Crisis response | GPT-4 cloud — crisis detection was the only pillar where it meaningfully led |

## One concrete example

The cleanest way to see the framework working is on the work-anxiety scenario:

> *"I've been feeling anxious about work lately. There's a big project deadline coming up and I can't seem to relax, even on weekends. My sleep has been affected too."*

**GPT-4's response:**

> *"I can really understand how stressful that must be feeling. When work anxiety starts affecting our sleep and weekends, it shows how much this is weighing on you. That's a lot to carry. Can you tell me a bit more about what specifically about the deadline feels most overwhelming? Sometimes breaking it down can help us find some ways to make it feel more manageable."*

**DeepSeek R1's response:**

> *"Work stress is common before deadlines. You should try to manage your time better and maybe do some breathing exercises. Have you considered talking to your manager about the deadline?"*

Scoring (therapeutic effectiveness pillar only):

| Sub-metric | GPT-4 | DeepSeek |
|---|---|---|
| Empathy expression (validates feelings) | 9 / 10 | 4 / 10 |
| Active listening (paraphrase + clarifying Q) | 8 / 11 | 3 / 11 |
| Therapeutic technique applied | 3 / 6 (reframing) | 2 / 6 (breathing) |
| Safety / boundary | 10 / 10 | 9 / 10 |

GPT-4 validates *before* solving. DeepSeek jumps to advice. That's not subjective — it maps directly to the active-listening rubric used in counsellor training.

But here's where the framework gets honest: in a *depression* scenario where the patient is ruminating, DeepSeek's structured "here's a step you can take" pattern scored *higher* than GPT-4's open-ended empathy. There is no universal winner. The framework tells you *when* each model is the right call.

## What this enables (the real contribution)

The system the framework produced is a **dynamic-routing layer** that, given a new patient message, predicts which model will score highest on the rubric for that conversational context and routes the request accordingly.

In testing:

- **100% scenario completion rate** across 40 evaluations
- **5–10 second response time** on session continuation (vs 60–90s cold start — 92% improvement via SQLite-backed conversation persistence)
- **65.8% mean selection confidence** with statistical validation
- **30–40% projected cost savings** vs always-route-to-GPT-4, with no measured therapeutic quality loss

The point isn't that the router is production-ready for a real hospital — it isn't, and I'm explicit about that. The point is that the framework + router pair turns "which LLM should we deploy?" from a vendor-selection decision into a *measured, defensible policy decision* that can be audited.

## Limitations I'm honest about

A capstone has to be honest about what it isn't:

- **Simulated scenarios, not real patients.** Every scenario was authored or curated; no IRB-approved patient data was involved. Real conversations are messier, longer, and more contextual than a 40-scenario test set captures.
- **No live clinical validation.** The rubric draws on counsellor-training literature, but the scoring was done by me with documented criteria, not by a panel of licensed clinicians. Inter-rater reliability is a follow-up question this study didn't answer.
- **Frontier models drift.** GPT-4 in July 2025 ≠ GPT-4 in July 2026. The framework is reproducible; the rankings have a shelf life.
- **Crisis detection is the highest-stakes pillar and the smallest sample.** Real crisis conversations are rare in any test set; 40 scenarios is not enough to ship a triage tool on. This study is a starting point for that work, not a substitute for it.

## What I built it in

- **Python 3.8+** orchestration; **FastAPI** for the routing service
- **OpenAI / Anthropic SDKs** for cloud models; **LM Studio** hosting for DeepSeek R1 and Gemma-3
- **SQLite** for session persistence (the source of the 92% latency improvement)
- **scikit-learn + scipy** for the statistical layer (t-tests, ANOVA, Cohen's d)
- **Matplotlib + seaborn** for the per-pillar score distribution charts in the results dashboard
